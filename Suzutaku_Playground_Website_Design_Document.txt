Suzutaku Playground
Website Design Document
Version 1.0

==================================================
1. Project Overview
==================================================

Project name:
Suzutaku Playground

Domain:
suzutakuplayground.com

Tagline:
Play with ideas.

Short description:
Small interactive games about Japan, science, and fun.

Japanese description:
日本・科学・遊びをテーマにした、短時間で楽しめるインタラクティブゲーム集。

Core concept:
- All content is presented as a game or interactive experience.
- Users should understand the idea by playing, not by reading long explanations.
- Each game should be easy to start and playable in a browser.
- The site should feel personal, curious, playful, and memorable.
- The site is inspired by interactive websites such as Neal.fun, but it must have its own visual identity and original games.

Primary goal:
Publish small web games regularly and grow Suzutaku Playground into a recognizable personal game brand.

Initial success target:
- Launch the website with a working home page.
- Publish at least one playable game.
- Get 100 users who are not personal acquaintances.
- Measure which games are opened, completed, and shared.

==================================================
2. Target Audience
==================================================

Primary audience:
- People who enjoy short browser games.
- Users interested in Japan.
- Students and adults interested in science.
- People looking for unusual, funny, or shareable online experiences.
- English-speaking international users.
- Japanese users.

Recommended age:
Approximately 10 years old and above.

Typical session:
- 30 seconds to 5 minutes per game.
- Users may play several games in one visit.

Platforms:
- Smartphone
- Tablet
- Desktop browser

Priority:
Mobile-first design, while remaining comfortable on desktop.

==================================================
3. Content Categories
==================================================

All published content is treated as a game.

Main categories:

1. Japan
Games about Japanese culture, society, food, school life, transportation, geography, and everyday life.

Examples:
- Spend ¥1000 at a Konbini
- Build Your Bento
- Japanese School Day
- Ride the Shinkansen
- Capsule Toy Machine
- Summer Festival
- Japanese Vending Machine
- How Far Is Japan?

2. Science
Games that make scientific ideas understandable through interaction.

Examples:
- How Small Is an Atom?
- Scale of the Universe
- Build an Atom
- Travel Through the Human Body
- Earth’s 4.6 Billion Years
- DNA Builder
- Gravity Playground
- Electricity Simulator

3. Fun
Funny, strange, experimental, or intentionally pointless games made mainly for entertainment.

Examples:
- Infinite Button
- Banana Physics
- Mischief of the Wind
- Push Everything
- Useless Machine
- How Long Can You Wait?
- Random Life Generator
- One Million Clicks

Category rules:
- Every game belongs to one primary category.
- A game may also have tags.
- Categories remain limited to Japan, Science, and Fun for the initial version.
- New categories should not be added until there are enough games to justify them.

==================================================
4. Brand Identity
==================================================

Brand name:
Suzutaku Playground

Brand position:
A personal playground of small interactive games.

Brand personality:
- Curious
- Playful
- Friendly
- Clever
- Slightly unusual
- Simple rather than corporate

Primary tagline:
Play with ideas.

Alternative supporting line:
Small interactive games about Japan, science, and fun.

Japanese supporting line:
遊びながら、ちょっと発見する。

Logo direction:
- Text-based logo at first.
- “Suzutaku Playground” should be clearly readable.
- Avoid overused Japanese symbols such as Mount Fuji, torii gates, or cherry blossoms as the main logo.
- A simple geometric symbol or playful “SP” mark may be developed later.
- The logo must work in black and white.

Visual style:
- Minimal
- Warm
- Modern
- Large typography
- Generous spacing
- Rounded cards
- Light animation
- No excessive visual clutter

Suggested color direction:
Background: warm off-white, such as #F7F4ED
Main text: near black, such as #1C1C1C
Primary accent: warm red, such as #E94B3C
Japan category accent: red or vermilion
Science category accent: blue or teal
Fun category accent: yellow, orange, or purple

Important:
Final color values should be tested for accessibility and sufficient contrast.

Typography:
- English: clean sans-serif
- Japanese: readable Japanese sans-serif
- Use a limited number of font weights.
- Avoid decorative fonts for body text.

==================================================
5. Language Design
==================================================

Initial languages:
- English
- Japanese

Recommended default:
English, because the project aims to reach international users.

Language switch:
- Clearly visible in the header.
- Simple “EN / 日本語” control.
- The selected language should remain active during the visit.
- URLs may later use /en/ and /ja/, but the first version may use a simple language state.

Translation policy:
- Game titles may differ slightly between English and Japanese when a direct translation sounds unnatural.
- Instructions should be short.
- Avoid machine-like translations.
- All essential buttons, game descriptions, and result screens should support both languages.

9. Game Card Design
==================================================

Each game card contains:

Required:
- Thumbnail
- Game title
- Category
- Short description
- Play status

Optional:
- Play time
- New badge
- Popular badge
- Language availability

Statuses:
- Play
- Coming Soon
- Prototype
- Updated

Card interaction:
- Entire card should be clickable when the game is available.
- Cards should have clear hover and focus states.
- Coming Soon cards should not look identical to playable cards.
- Avoid excessive text.

Example:

Description: Choose what to buy without going over your budget.

==================================================
10. Game Page Template
==================================================

Each game page should have a reusable structure.

Recommended structure:

1. Minimal game header
- Back to Playground
- Game title
- Language switch
- Sound control, if required

2. Game area
- Main interactive experience
- Full-width where appropriate
- Optimized for touch and mouse

3. Result screen
- Result or score
- Short personalized message
- Replay button
- Share button
- Next game recommendation

4. Information section
- Very short explanation
- Credits
- Data sources for science or factual games
- Last updated date where relevant

5. Related games
- Two or three recommended games

Game design rule:
The user should begin playing within a few seconds.

Avoid:
- Long tutorials
- Account registration before play
- Intrusive advertising
- Pop-ups before the game begins

15. Navigation and User Flow
==================================================

Ideal first visit:
1. User lands on the home page.
2. User understands the concept within five seconds.
3. User sees a featured game.
4. User clicks Play.
5. User begins within a few seconds.
6. User finishes and receives a result.
7. User shares or selects another game.

Main navigation goal:
Reduce the number of steps between discovery and play.

Maximum recommended clicks:
- Home to any game: one or two clicks
- Game result to next game: one click

==================================================
16. Responsive Design
==================================================

Mobile-first requirements:
- Touch targets should be large.
- Text must remain readable without zooming.
- No horizontal page scrolling unless intentionally part of a game.
- Game controls should work with one finger where possible.
- Cards should use one column on narrow screens.
- Large games should fit within the viewport.
- Avoid tiny icons.

Desktop:
- Use wider layouts but do not stretch text excessively.
- Keep content centered with a maximum width.
- Use hover effects as enhancement only.

==================================================
17. Accessibility
==================================================

Minimum requirements:
- Sufficient text contrast
- Keyboard-accessible navigation
- Visible focus states
- Alt text for meaningful images
- Buttons must use clear labels
- Do not rely only on color to communicate status
- Allow reduced animation for users who prefer it
- Avoid rapid flashing
- Add captions or text alternatives for important audio information
- Games should explain controls in simple language

==================================================
18. Animation and Sound
==================================================

Animation principles:
- Use animation to show cause and effect.
- Keep transitions quick.
- Avoid animation that delays interaction.
- Respect reduced-motion settings.

Sound principles:
- Sound off or restrained by default depending on the game.
- Always provide a mute control when sound is used.
- Do not autoplay loud audio.
- Use short and recognizable feedback sounds.

==================================================
19. Technical Architecture
==================================================

Recommended initial stack:
- React
- TypeScript
- Vite
- CSS
- GitHub
- Vercel

Optional libraries:
- React Router for pages
- Framer Motion for controlled animation
- Canvas or SVG for interactive graphics
- Three.js only for games that genuinely need 3D

Avoid at launch:
- Database
- User accounts
- Complex backend
- Comment system
- Global leaderboard
- Heavy game engines for simple 2D experiences

Game engines:
Unity or Godot Web exports may be used later for selected games, but ordinary web technologies should be preferred for small games because they load faster.

==================================================
24. Legal and Safety Requirements
==================================================

Required before wider launch:
- Privacy policy
- Cookie notice only if required by the selected analytics or advertising tools
- Clear copyright notice
- Credits for external assets
- Licenses for fonts, sounds, images, and code libraries

Content:
- Avoid copyrighted characters and brands without permission.
- Use fictional product designs where appropriate.
- Do not directly copy Neal.fun games.
- Concepts may be inspired by interactive web design, but graphics, writing, code, and game mechanics should be original.
- Scientific games should cite reliable data sources.
- Clearly identify simulations and approximations.

25. Monetization Plan
==================================================

Phase 1:
- No intrusive ads
- Focus on quality, publishing, and traffic
- Optional support link

Phase 2:
- Small support or donation button
- Light advertising in non-game areas
- Sponsorship opportunities

Phase 3:
- Sponsored educational games
- Museum, school, tourism, or business collaborations
- Premium game collections
- Educational licensing
- Related downloadable games or assets

Revenue must not reduce usability.

Avoid:
- Full-screen ads before play
- Forced account creation
- Paywalls on the first games
- Misleading buttons

29. Final Design Direction
==================================================

Suzutaku Playground should not look like a large commercial game portal.

It should feel like:
- A personal collection
- A digital toy box
- A place for curiosity
- A site where every card leads to a small surprise

The central promise is:

Play with ideas.

Every decision should support:
- Fast access
- Simple interaction
- Clear feedback
- Memorable results
- Easy movement to the next game
